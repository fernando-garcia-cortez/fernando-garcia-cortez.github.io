A deep exploration of complex scalar fields

July XXth, 2024.

Introduction

The basics of (real) scalar field theory and its quantization are heavily covered in various books and review articles. Brief comments are usually made on its complex brother: field theory of a complex scalar field.

In this blog post I will be going over, in detail, the basics of having a complex scalar field and its quantization. A final comment will be made on the ground state of the theory. This post was heavily inspired by a Problem in Mark Srednicki's QFT book.

As a classical field

Let's make the theory concrete by writing down the its Lagrangian:

\[\mathcal{L}=-\partial ^{\mu }\phi ^{\dagger}\partial _{\mu }\phi -m^2 \phi ^{\dagger}\phi +\Omega _0 \]

Where $\Omega _0 $ is a constant. The equation of motion of a real scalar field analogous to the one above is the Klein-Gordon equation. It is straight forward to show that both $\phi $ and $\phi ^{\dagger} $ follow the same equation.

If we plug the Lagrangian into the Euler-Lagrange equation for the $\phi $ field, we find:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \phi } &= \partial _{\mu } \left( \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial (\partial _{\mu }\phi )} \right) \\ -m^2 \phi ^{\dagger} &= \partial _{\mu } \left( -\partial ^{\mu }\phi ^{\dagger} \right) \\ \partial _{\mu }\partial ^{\mu }\phi ^{\dagger}-m^2 \phi ^{\dagger} &= 0\\ \left( \partial _{\mu }\partial ^{\mu }-m^2 \right) \phi ^{\dagger} &= 0 \end{align*}

And if we do it for the $\phi ^{\dagger} $ field, we find:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \phi ^{\dagger}} &= \partial _{\mu } \left( \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial (\partial _{\mu }\phi ^{\dagger})} \right) \\ -m^2 \phi &= \partial _{\mu } \left( -\partial ^{\mu }\phi \right) \\ \partial _{\mu }\partial ^{\mu }\phi -m^2 \phi &= 0\\ \left( \partial _{\mu }\partial ^{\mu }-m^2 \right) \phi &= 0 \end{align*}

Where it is relevant to remark that we treat $\phi $ and $\phi ^{\dagger} $ as independent fields. From a canonical point of view (to be worked out later on), the Hamiltonian (density) plays a central role, and as such we need to find the canonical momenta of the theory. We see that

\begin{align*} \Pi _{\phi }(x) &= \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{\phi }} \\ &= \dot{\phi }^{\dagger}(x) \end{align*}

And

\begin{align*} \Pi _{\phi ^{\dagger}}(x) &= \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{\phi }^{\dagger}} \\ &= \dot{\phi }(x) \end{align*}

So the Hamiltonian (density) reads:

\begin{align*} \mathcal{H} &= \Pi _{\phi }\dot{\phi }+\Pi _{\phi ^{\dagger}}\dot{\phi }^{\dagger}-\mathcal{L} \\ &= \dot{\phi }^{\dagger}\dot{\phi }+\dot{\phi }\dot{\phi }^{\dagger}+\partial ^{\mu }\phi ^{\dagger}\partial _{\mu }\phi +m^2 \phi ^{\dagger}\phi -\Omega _0 \\ &= \dot{\phi }^{\dagger}\dot{\phi }+\dot{\phi }\dot{\phi }^{\dagger}+\eta ^{\mu \nu }\partial _{\mu }\phi ^{\dagger}\partial _{\mu }\phi +m^2 \phi ^{\dagger}\phi -\Omega _0 \\ &= \dot{\phi }^{\dagger}\dot{\phi }+\dot{\phi }\dot{\phi }^{\dagger}+ \left( -\dot{\phi }^{\dagger}\dot{\phi }+\nabla \phi ^{\dagger}\nabla \phi \right) +m^2 \phi ^{\dagger}\phi -\Omega _0 \\ &= \Pi _{\phi ^{\dagger}}\Pi _{\phi }+\nabla \phi ^{\dagger}\nabla \phi +m^2 \phi ^{\dagger}\phi -\Omega _0 \\ \end{align*}

Now, let's suppose we expand $\phi $ in terms of modes:

\[\phi (x)=\int \tilde{dk} \left( a(\mathbf{k})e^{ikx}+b^{\dagger}(\mathbf{k})e^{-ikx} \right) \]

Where

\[\tilde{dk}=\frac{d^3 k}{(2\pi )^3 2\omega } \]

It is relevant to remark that $kx\equiv \mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{x}-\omega t $.

The immediate question is what are $a(\mathbf{k}) $ and $b^{\dagger}(\mathbf{k}) $. Here it is relevant to note that in the case that $\phi $ is real, $a $ and $b $ are related to each other and we get the usual expressions. In this case (complex $\phi $) we will have to work it out.
