Elementary Functions.

Elementary properties of complex numbers.

Triangle inequality

\[|z_1 +z_2 |\leq |z_1 |+|z_2 | \]

Reverse triangle inequality

\[|z_1 +z_2 |\geq ||z_1 |-|z_2 || \]

Properties of conjugation:

\begin{align*} \overline{z_1 \pm z_2 } &= \overline{z_1 }\pm \overline{z_2 } \\ \overline{z_1 z_2 } &= \overline{z_1 }\cdot \overline{z_2 }\\ \overline{ \left( \frac{z_1 }{z_2 } \right) } &= \frac{\overline{z_1 }}{\overline{z_2 }} \end{align*}

Identities of conjugation:

\begin{align*} \text{Re} (z) &= \frac{z+\overline{z}}{2} \\ \text{Im} (z) &= \frac{z-\overline{z}}{2i} \\ z\overline{z} &= |z|^2 \end{align*}

Properties of moduli:

\begin{align*} |z_1 z_2 | &= |z_1 ||z_2 | \\ \bigg| \frac{z_1 }{z_2 }\bigg| &= \frac{|z_1 |}{|z_2 |}\ \end{align*}

Properties of Arg:

\begin{align*} \text{arg} (z_1 z_2 ) &= \text{arg} (z_1 )+\text{arg} (z_2 ) \\ \text{arg} (z_1 /z_2 ) &= \text{arg} (z_1 )+\text{arg} (z_2 ) \end{align*}

Complex plane as a metric space.

Moduli of complex numbers defines a metric on $\mathbb{C} $:

\[d(z_1 ,z_2 )=|z_1 -z_2 | \]

The $\epsilon $-neighborhood of a complex number $z_0 $ is the set of points $z $ that satisfy

\[|z-z_0 |<\epsilon \]

The deleted neighborhood of $z_0 $ is:

\[0<|z-z_0 |<\epsilon \]

$z_0 $ is an interior point of a set $S $ of complex numbers if there is a neighborhood of $z_0 $ contained inside $S $.

$z _0 $ is an exterior point of $S $ if there is a neighborhood of $z_0 $ contained outside of $S $.

$z_0 $ is a boundary point if it is not an interior nor an exterior point. Every neighborhood of a boundary point contains at least one element in $S $ and one in $S^C $.

The set of boundary points of a set is known as its boundary.

A set $S $ is said to be open if it contains none of its boundary points. Alternatively, $S $ is open iff all of its points are interior points.

A set $S $ is said to be closed if it contains all of its boundary points.

The closure of a set is the union of the set and its boundary.

An open set is said to be connected if each pair of points can be joined by a polygonal line of finite segments.

A domain is a nonempty connected open set.

A set $S $ is bounded if every point of $S $ lies inside some circle $|z|=R $. A set is unbounded if it is non bounded.

$z_0 $ is known as an accumulation point of a set $S $ if each deleted neighborhood of $z_0 $ contains at least one point of $S $.

Theorem: A set is closed iff it contains all of its accumulation points.

Limits and continuity.

Images of entire domains are known as ranges of functions/mappings.

Although the image of a point is unique, the inverse image in the range can consist of one or more points.

The action on the domain of many functions can be understood through translations, rotations, and reflections.

The limit of $f(z) $ as $z\rightarrow z_0 $ is $w_0 $ if for each positive number $\epsilon $, there is a positive number $\delta $ such that

\[|f(z)-w_0|<\epsilon \;\text{whenever} \; 0<|z-z_0 |<\delta \]

"For each $\epsilon $-neighborhood of $w_0 $ there is a deleted $\delta $-neighborhood of $z_0 $ such that every point in it has an image $w $ in the $\epsilon $-neighborhood."

Theorem: When a limit of a function exist at a point, it is unique.

Theorem: Let $f(z)=u+iv $ for real functions $u,v $, and $z_0 =x_0 +iy_0 $, $w_0 =u_0 +iv_0 $. Then $\lim_{z\to z_0 }f(z)=w_0 $ iff $\lim_{(x,y)\to (x_0 ,y_0 )}u(x,y)=u_0 $ and $\lim_{(x,y)\to (x_0 ,y_0 )}v(x,y)=v_0 $.

Theorem: Let $\lim_{z\to z_0 }f(z)=w_0 $ and $\lim_{z\to z_0 }F(z)=W_0 $. Then

\begin{align*} \lim_{z\to z_0 } \left[ f(z)+F(z) \right] &= w_0 +W_0 \\ \lim_{z\to z_0 } \left[ f(z)F(z) \right] &= w_0 W_0 \\ \lim_{z\to z_0 }\frac{f(z)}{F(z)} &= \frac{w_0 }{W_0}\tag{$W_0 \neq 0 $} \end{align*}

Elementary limits:

\begin{align*} \lim_{z\to z_0 }c &= c\tag{For constant $c $} \\ \lim_{z\to z_0 }z^n &= z_0 ^n \tag{For $n=1,2,3,\cdots $} \end{align*}

The set of complex numbers in addition to the point at infinity is known as the extended complex plane.

Theorem: Limits regarding infinity:

\begin{align*} \lim_{z\to z_0 }f(z)=\infty \; &\Leftrightarrow \;\lim_{z\to z_0 }\frac{1}{f(z)}=0 \\ \lim_{z\to \infty }f(z)=w_0 \; &\Leftrightarrow \; \lim_{z\to 0}f(1/z)=w_0 \\ \lim_{z\to \infty }f(z)=\infty &\Leftrightarrow \lim_{z\to 0}\frac{1}{f(1/z)}=0 \end{align*}

A function $f $ is said to be continuous at a point $z_0 $ if $\lim_{z\to z_0 }f(z) $ exists and is equal to $f(z_0 ) $. In other words, for each positive number $\epsilon $, there is a positive $\delta $ such that $|f(z)-f(z_0 )|< \epsilon $ whenever $ |z-z_ 0 | < \delta $.

A function $f $ is said to be continuous in a region $R $ if it is continuous at every point in $R $.

Theorem: Let $f,g $ be two continuous functions at $z_0 $. Then $f(z_0 )+g(z_0 ) $, $f(z_0 )g(z_0 ) $, $f(z_0 )/g(z_0 ) $ are also continuous at $z_0 $.

Theorem: Let $f,g $ be two continuous functions. Then their composition $f\circ g $ is also continuous.

Theorem: If a function $f(z) $ is continuous and nonzero at a point $z_0 $, then $f(z) \neq 0$ throughout some neighborhood of that point.

Theorem: The function $f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y) $ is continuous at a point iff its component functions are continuous there.

Theorem: If a function $f $ is continuous throughout a region $R $ that is both closed and bounded, there exists a nonnegative real number $M $ such that $|f(z)|\leq M $ for all points $z \in R $, and equality holds for at least one such $z $. We say that $f $ is bounded on $R $.

Derivatives.

The derivative of $f $ at $z_0 $ is the limit

\[f'(z_0 )=\lim_{z\to z_0 }\frac{f(z)-f(z_0 )}{z-z_0 } \equiv \lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z_0 +\Delta z)-f(z_0 )}{\Delta z}\]

If $f'(z_0 ) $, then $f $ is said to be differentiable at $z_0 $.

Theorem: The existence of the derivative of a function at a point implies the continuity of the function at that point.

Cauchy-Riemann equations:

\begin{align*} u_x(x_0 ,y_0 ) &= v_y(x_0 ,y_0) \\ u_y(x_0 ,y_0 ) &= -v_x(x_0 ,y_0) \end{align*}

Theorem: Suppose that $f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y) $. Suppose that $f'(z) $ exists at a point $z_0 =x_0 +iy_0 $. Then the first-order partial derivatives of $u,v $ must exist at $(x_0 ,y_0 ) $ and they must satisfy the Cauchy-Riemann equations there. Further:

\[f'(z_0 )=u_x+iv_x \]

Theorem: (sufficient condition for differentiability) Let $f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y) $ be defined throughout some $\epsilon $-neighborhood of a point $z_0 =x_0 +iy_0 $, and suppose that (1) the first order partial derivatives of the functions $u,v $ with respect to $x,y $ exist everywhere in the neighborhood, and (2) those partial derivatives are continuous at $(x_0 ,y_0 ) $ and satisfy the Cauchy-Riemann equations there. Then $f'(z_0 ) $ exists and is equal to $f'(z_0 )=u_x+iv_x $ evaluated at $(x_0 ,y_0 ) $.

Cauchy-Riemann in polar form:

\begin{align*} ru_r &= v_{\theta } \\ u_{\theta } &= -rv_r \\ f'(z_0 ) &= e^{-i\theta }(u_r+iv_r) \end{align*}

Analytic Functions.

A function $f $ is said to be analytic at $z_0 $ if it ha a derivative at each point in some neighborhood of $z_0 $. Also known as regular or holomorphic.

$f $ is analytic in an open set if it has a derivative everywhere in that set.

An entire function is an analytic function at each point in the entire finite plane.

$z_0 $ is said to be a singular point (or singularity) of $f $ if it fails to be analytic there but is analytic at some point in every neighborhood of $z_0 $.

Theorem: Sums, products, and quotients of analytic functions are analytic as well. Composition of two analytic functions results in an analytic function.

Theorem: If $f'(z)=0 $ everywhere in a domain $D $, then $f(z) $ must be constant throughout $D $.

Theorem: If both $f $ and $\overline{f} $ are analytic in some domain $D $, then $f $ must be constant throughout $D $.

A real-valued function $H(x,y) $ (two real variables) is said to be harmonic in a given domain if (1) it has continuous partial derivatives of the first and second order, as well as (2) satisfying the PDE known as Laplace's equation:

\[H_{xx}+H_{yy}=0 \]

Theorem: If a function $f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y) $ is analytic in $D $, then its component functions $u,v $ are harmonic in $D $.

Two functions $u,v $ are harmonic conjugates of each other if they are harmonic in some domain $D $ and their first-order partial derivatives satisfy the CR equations throughout the domain.

Theorem: A function $f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y) $ is analytic in a domain $D $ iff $v $ is a harmonic conjugate of $u $.

Lemma: Suppose that (1) a function $f $ is analytic throughout a domain $D $, and (2) $f(z) =0 $ at each point $z $ of a domain or line segment contained in $D $. Then $f(z)\equiv 0 $ in $D $, that is, $f(z) $ is identically equal to zero throughout $D $.

Theorem: A function that is analytic in a domain $D $ is uniquely determined over $D $ by its values in a domain, or along a line segment, contained in $D $.

Theorem: Suppose that a function $f $ is analytic in some domain $D $ which contains a segment of the $x $ axis and whose lwoer half is the reflection of the upper half with respect to that axis. Then $\overline{f(z)}=f(\overline{z}) $ for each point $z $ in the domain iff $f(x) $ is real for each point $x $ on the segment.

Elementary Functions.

Properties of the complex exponential function:

  • $e^{z_1 }e^{z_2 }=e^{z_1 +z_2 } $.
  • $e^{z_1 }/e^{z_2 }=e^{z_1 -z_2 } $.
  • $e^{z} $ is entire, further: $de^{z}/dz=e^{z} $.
  • $e^{z}\neq 0 $ for any complex $z $.
  • $e^{z} $ is periodic: $e^{z+2\pi i}=e^{z} $.
  • Contrary to the real counterpart $e^{x} $, $e^{z} $ can take negative values.

Properties of the complex logarithmic function:

  • If $z=re^{i\Theta } $ for $-\pi <\Theta \leq \pi $, then $\log z = \ln r + i (\Theta +2n\pi ) $ for $n \in \mathbb{Z} $.
  • $\log (e^{z})=z+2n\pi i $ for $n \in \mathbb{Z} $.
  • The principal value corresponds to the $n=0 $ case.
  • We can find logs of negative real numbers now.

A single-valued function can be constructed with $\log z = \ln r + i\theta $ where $\alpha <\theta <\alpha +2\pi $ for a fixed $\alpha $. Points at $\alpha =0 $ break continuity. This function is continuous and analytic in $r>0 $, $\alpha <\theta <\alpha +2\pi $.

$d\log z / dz= 1/re^{i\theta }=1/z $, where $z=re^{i\theta } $, $\theta =\Theta +2n\pi $, $\Theta =\text{Arg} z $.

A branch (from a multiple-valued function) is any single-valued function $F $ that is analytic in some domain at each point $ z$ of which the value $F(z) $ is one of the values of $f $.

The log principal branch is

\[\text{log} z=\ln r + i\Theta \tag{$r>0,-\pi <\Theta <\pi $} \]

A branch cut is a portion of a line or curve that is introduced in order to define a branch $F $ on a multiple-valued function $f $. Points on the branch cut are singular points.

A point is said to be a branch point if it is a point common to all branch cuts of a function.

Properties of logarithms:

\begin{align*} \log (z_1 z_2 ) &= \log (z_1 )+\log (z_2 ) \\ \log (z_1 /z_2 ) &= \log z_1 -\log z_2 \\ z^n &= e^{n \cdot \log z}\tag{$n\in \mathbb{Z} , z\neq 0 $} \\ z^{1/n} &= \exp \left( \frac{1}{n}\log z \right) \tag{$n=1,2,\cdots ,\; z\neq 0 $} \end{align*}

$z^{c} $ for complex $z,c $ is defined as

\[z^{c}=e^{c \log z} \]

So complex exponents are multi-valued functions.

In the $\alpha $-branch $\log z = \ln r + i\theta $ for $\alpha <\theta <\alpha +2\pi $, $z^{c}=\exp \left( c \log z \right) $ is single-valued and analytic. This branch of $z^{c} $ has derivative:

\[\frac{d}{dz}z^{c}=cz^{c-1}\]

The principal value is given by

\[\text{P.V.} \;z^{c}=e^{c\text{Log} \;z} \]

Sine and cosine are defined as

\begin{align*} \sin \left( z \right) &= \frac{e^{iz}-e^{-iz}}{2i} \\ \cos \left( z \right) &= \frac{e^{iz}+e^{-iz}}{2} \end{align*}

And have derivatives

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dz}\sin \left( z \right) &= \cos \left( z \right) \\ \frac{d}{dz}\cos \left( z \right) &= -\sin \left( z \right) \end{align*}

And odd/even properties:

\begin{align*} \sin \left( -z \right) &= -\sin \left( z \right) \\ \cos \left( -z \right) &= \cos \left( z \right) \end{align*}

Trig identities:

\begin{align*} \sin \left( z_1 +z_2 \right) &= \sin \left( z_1 \right) \cos \left( z_2 \right) +\cos \left( z_1 \right) \sin \left( z_2 \right) \\ \cos \left( z_1 +z_2 \right) &= \cos \left( z_1 \right) \cos \left( z_2 \right) -\sin \left( z_1 \right) \sin \left( z_2 \right) \\ \sin \left( 2z \right) &= 2\sin \left( z \right) \cos \left( z \right) \\ \cos \left( 2z \right) &= \cos ^2 z-\sin ^2 z \\ \sin \left( z+\frac{\pi }{2} \right) &= \cos \left( z \right) \\ \sin \left( z-\frac{\pi }{2} \right) &= -\cos \left( z \right) \\ \sin ^2 z+\cos ^2 z &= 1 \\ \sin \left( z+2\pi \right) &= \sin \left( z \right) \\ \sin \left( z+\pi \right) &= -\sin \left( z \right) \\ \cos \left( z+2\pi \right) &= \cos \left( z \right) \\ \cos \left( z+\pi \right) &= -\cos \left( z \right) \end{align*}

The hyperbolic functions are defined as

\begin{align*} \sinh z &= \frac{e^{z}-e^{-z}}{2} \\ \cosh z &= \frac{e^{z}+e^{-z}}{2} \end{align*}

So that

\begin{align*} \sin \left( iy \right) &= i \sinh y \\ \cos \left( iy \right) &= \cosh y \\ \sin \left( z \right) &= \sin \left( x \right) \cosh (y)+i \cos \left( x \right) \sinh (y)\\ \cos \left( z \right) &= \cos \left( x \right) \cosh y - i \sin \left( x \right) \sinh y \\ |\sin \left( z \right) |^2 &= \sin ^2 x + \sinh ^2 y \\ |\cos \left( z \right) |^2 &= \cos ^2 z + \sinh ^2 y \end{align*}

Other trigonometric functions:

\begin{align*} \tan \left( z \right) &= \frac{\sin \left( z \right) }{\cos \left( z \right) } & \frac{d}{dz}\tan \left( z \right) &= \sec ^2 z \\ \sec \left( z \right) &= \frac{1}{\cos \left( z \right) } & \frac{d}{dz}\sec \left( z \right) &= \sec \left( z \right) \tan \left( z \right) \\ \cot \left( z \right) &= \frac{\cos \left( z \right) }{\sin \left( z \right) } & \frac{d}{dz}\cot \left( z \right) &= -\csc ^2 (z) \\ \csc (z) &= \frac{1}{\sin \left( z \right) } & \frac{d}{dz}\csc (z) &= -\csc (z)\cot \left( z \right) \end{align*}

Note that $\tan \left( z \right) ,\sec \left( z \right) $ are analytic except at the singularities (zeros of $\cos \left( z \right) $) $z=\frac{\pi }{2}+n\pi $ for $n \in \mathbb{Z} $.

$\cot \left( z \right) ,\csc (z) $ have singularities at the zeros of $\sin \left( z \right) $: $z=n\pi $ for $n \in \mathbb{Z} $.

They are all periodic in some way.

More hyperbolic identities:

\begin{align*} - \sinh (iz) &= \sin \left( z \right) \\ \cosh (iz) &= \cos \left( z \right) \\ - \sin \left( iz \right) &= \sinh (z)\\ \cos \left( iz \right) &= \cosh (z)\\ \sinh (-z) &= -\sinh (z)\\ \cosh (-z) &= \cos \left( z \right) \\ \cosh ^2 z - \sinh ^2 z &= 1\\ \sinh (z_1 +z_2 ) &= \sinh z_1 \cosh z_2 +\cosh z_1 \sinh z_2 \\ \cosh (z_1 +z_2 ) &= \cosh z_1 \cosh z_2 +\sinh z_1 \sinh z_2 \\ \sinh z &= \sinh x \cos \left( y \right) +i\cosh x \sin \left( y \right) \\ \cosh z &= \cosh x \cos \left( y \right) +i \sinh x \sin \left( y \right) \\ |\sinh z|^2 &= \sinh ^2 x + \sin ^2 y \\ |\cosh z|^2 &= \sinh ^2 x + \cos ^2 y \end{align*}

More hyperbolic functions:

\begin{align*} \tanh z &= \frac{\sinh z}{\cosh z} & \frac{d}{dz}\tanh z &= \text{sech} ^2 z\\ \coth z &= \frac{1}{\tanh z} & \frac{d}{dz}\coth z &= -\text{csch} ^2 z\\ \text{sech} z &= \frac{1}{\cosh z} & \frac{d}{dz}\text{sech} z &= -\text{sech} z \tanh z\\ \text{csch} z &= \frac{1}{\sinh z} & \frac{d}{dz}\text{csch} z &= -\text{csch} z \coth z\\ \end{align*}

Inverse trig functions:

\begin{align*} \sin ^{-1} z &= -i \log \left[ iz+(1-z^2 )^{1/2} \right] & \frac{d}{dz}\sin ^{-1} z &= \frac{1}{(1-z^2 )^{1/2}}\\ \cos ^{-1} z &= -i \log \left[ z+i(1-z^2 )^{1/2} \right] & \frac{d}{dz}\cos ^{-1} z &= \frac{-1}{(1-z^2 )^{1/2}}\\ \tan ^{-1} z &= \frac{i}{2}\log \frac{i+z}{i-z} & \frac{d}{dz}\tan ^{-1} z &= \frac{1}{1+z^2 } \end{align*}

Inverse hyperbolic trig functions:

\begin{align*} \sinh ^{-1} z &= \log \left[ z+(z^2 +1)^{1/2} \right] \\ \cosh ^{-1} z &= \log \left[ z+(z^2 -1)^{1/2} \right] \\ \tanh ^{-1} z &= \frac{1}{2}\log \frac{1+z}{1-z} \end{align*}
