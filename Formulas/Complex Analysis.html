Elementary properties of complex numbers.

Triangle inequality

\[|z_1 +z_2 |\leq |z_1 |+|z_2 | \]

Reverse triangle inequality

\[|z_1 +z_2 |\geq ||z_1 |-|z_2 || \]

Properties of conjugation:

\begin{align*} \overline{z_1 \pm z_2 } &= \overline{z_1 }\pm \overline{z_2 } \\ \overline{z_1 z_2 } &= \overline{z_1 }\cdot \overline{z_2 }\\ \overline{ \left( \frac{z_1 }{z_2 } \right) } &= \frac{\overline{z_1 }}{\overline{z_2 }} \end{align*}

Identities of conjugation:

\begin{align*} \text{Re} (z) &= \frac{z+\overline{z}}{2} \\ \text{Im} (z) &= \frac{z-\overline{z}}{2i} \\ z\overline{z} &= |z|^2 \end{align*}

Properties of moduli:

\begin{align*} |z_1 z_2 | &= |z_1 ||z_2 | \\ \bigg| \frac{z_1 }{z_2 }\bigg| &= \frac{|z_1 |}{|z_2 |}\ \end{align*}

Properties of Arg:

\begin{align*} \text{arg} (z_1 z_2 ) &= \text{arg} (z_1 )+\text{arg} (z_2 ) \\ \text{arg} (z_1 /z_2 ) &= \text{arg} (z_1 )+\text{arg} (z_2 ) \end{align*}

Complex plane as a metric space.

Moduli of complex numbers defines a metric on $\mathbb{C} $:

\[d(z_1 ,z_2 )=|z_1 -z_2 | \]

The $\epsilon $-neighborhood of a complex number $z_0 $ is the set of points $z $ that satisfy

\[|z-z_0 |<\epsilon \]

The deleted neighborhood of $z_0 $ is:

\[0<|z-z_0 |<\epsilon \]

$z_0 $ is an interior point of a set $S $ of complex numbers if there is a neighborhood of $z_0 $ contained inside $S $.

$z _0 $ is an exterior point of $S $ if there is a neighborhood of $z_0 $ contained outside of $S $.

$z_0 $ is a boundary point if it is not an interior nor an exterior point. Every neighborhood of a boundary point contains at least one element in $S $ and one in $S^C $.

The set of boundary points of a set is known as its boundary.

A set $S $ is said to be open if it contains none of its boundary points. Alternatively, $S $ is open iff all of its points are interior points.

A set $S $ is said to be closed if it contains all of its boundary points.

The closure of a set is the union of the set and its boundary.

An open set is said to be connected if each pair of points can be joined by a polygonal line of finite segments.

A domain is a nonempty connected open set.

A set $S $ is bounded if every point of $S $ lies inside some circle $|z|=R $. A set is unbounded if it is non bounded.

$z_0 $ is known as an accumulation point of a set $S $ if each deleted neighborhood of $z_0 $ contains at least one point of $S $.

Theorem: A set is closed iff it contains all of its accumulation points.

Limits and continuity.

Images of entire domains are known as ranges of functions/mappings.

Although the image of a point is unique, the inverse image in the range can consist of one or more points.

The action on the domain of many functions can be understood through translations, rotations, and reflections.

The limit of $f(z) $ as $z\rightarrow z_0 $ is $w_0 $ if for each positive number $\epsilon $, there is a positive number $\delta $ such that

\[|f(z)-w_0|<\epsilon \;\text{whenever} \; 0<|z-z_0 |<\delta \]

"For each $\epsilon $-neighborhood of $w_0 $ there is a deleted $\delta $-neighborhood of $z_0 $ such that every point in it has an image $w $ in the $\epsilon $-neighborhood."

Theorem: When a limit of a function exist at a point, it is unique.

Theorem: Let $f(z)=u+iv $ for real functions $u,v $, and $z_0 =x_0 +iy_0 $, $w_0 =u_0 +iv_0 $. Then $\lim_{z\to z_0 }f(z)=w_0 $ iff $\lim_{(x,y)\to (x_0 ,y_0 )}u(x,y)=u_0 $ and $\lim_{(x,y)\to (x_0 ,y_0 )}v(x,y)=v_0 $.

Theorem: Let $\lim_{z\to z_0 }f(z)=w_0 $ and $\lim_{z\to z_0 }F(z)=W_0 $. Then

\begin{align*} \lim_{z\to z_0 } \left[ f(z)+F(z) \right] &= w_0 +W_0 \\ \lim_{z\to z_0 } \left[ f(z)F(z) \right] &= w_0 W_0 \\ \lim_{z\to z_0 }\frac{f(z)}{F(z)} &= \frac{w_0 }{W_0}\tag{$W_0 \neq 0 $} \end{align*}

Elementary limits:

\begin{align*} \lim_{z\to z_0 }c &= c\tag{For constant $c $} \\ \lim_{z\to z_0 }z^n &= z_0 ^n \tag{For $n=1,2,3,\cdots $} \end{align*}

The set of complex numbers in addition to the point at infinity is known as the extended complex plane.

Theorem: Limits regarding infinity:

\begin{align*} \lim_{z\to z_0 }f(z)=\infty \; &\Leftrightarrow \;\lim_{z\to z_0 }\frac{1}{f(z)}=0 \\ \lim_{z\to \infty }f(z)=w_0 \; &\Leftrightarrow \; \lim_{z\to 0}f(1/z)=w_0 \\ \lim_{z\to \infty }f(z)=\infty &\Leftrightarrow \lim_{z\to 0}\frac{1}{f(1/z)}=0 \end{align*}

A function $f $ is said to be continuous at a point $z_0 $ if $\lim_{z\to z_0 }f(z) $ exists and is equal to $f(z_0 ) $. In other words, for each positive number $\epsilon $, there is a positive $\delta $ such that $|f(z)-f(z_0 )|< \epsilon $ whenever $ |z-z_ 0 | < \delta $.

A function $f $ is said to be continuous in a region $R $ if it is continuous at every point in $R $.

Theorem: Let $f,g $ be two continuous functions at $z_0 $. Then $f(z_0 )+g(z_0 ) $, $f(z_0 )g(z_0 ) $, $f(z_0 )/g(z_0 ) $ are also continuous at $z_0 $.

Theorem: Let $f,g $ be two continuous functions. Then their composition $f\circ g $ is also continuous.

Theorem: If a function $f(z) $ is continuous and nonzero at a point $z_0 $, then $f(z) \neq 0$ throughout some neighborhood of that point.

Theorem: The function $f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y) $ is continuous at a point iff its component functions are continuous there.

Theorem: If a function $f $ is continuous throughout a region $R $ that is both closed and bounded, there exists a nonnegative real number $M $ such that $|f(z)|\leq M $ for all points $z \in R $, and equality holds for at least one such $z $. We say that $f $ is bounded on $R $.

Derivatives.

The derivative of $f $ at $z_0 $ is the limit

\[f'(z_0 )=\lim_{z\to z_0 }\frac{f(z)-f(z_0 )}{z-z_0 } \equiv \lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z_0 +\Delta z)-f(z_0 )}{\Delta z}\]

If $f'(z_0 ) $, then $f $ is said to be differentiable at $z_0 $.

Theorem: The existence of the derivative of a function at a point implies the continuity of the function at that point.

Cauchy-Riemann equations:

\begin{align*} u_x(x_0 ,y_0 ) &= v_y(x_0 ,y_0) \\ u_y(x_0 ,y_0 ) &= -v_x(x_0 ,y_0) \end{align*}

Theorem: Suppose that $f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y) $. Suppose that $f'(z) $ exists at a point $z_0 =x_0 +iy_0 $. Then the first-order partial derivatives of $u,v $ must exist at $(x_0 ,y_0 ) $ and they must satisfy the Cauchy-Riemann equations there. Further:

\[f'(z_0 )=u_x+iv_x \]
