Metric spaces.

Def. Let $X $ be a set. A metric $d $ on $X $ is a real function such that $d:X\times X\rightarrow \mathbb{R} ^{+} $ such that $\forall x,y,z \in X $:

  1. Positiveness: $d(x,y)\geq 0 $.
  2. $d(x,y)=0 $ iff $x=y $.
  3. Symmetric: $d(x,y)=d(y,x) $.
  4. Triangle inequality: $d(x,z)\leq d(x,y)+d(y,z) $

Lemma Let $(X,d) $ be a metric space. Let $Y\subset X $ be a subset. Then $(Y,d|_Y) $ is also a metric space.

Definition An open ball in a metric space $(X,d) $ of radius $r $ centered at $x $ is the set $B(x,r)=\left\lbrace y\in X|d(x,y) < r \right\rbrace $ .

Definition Let $(X,d) $ be a metric space. Let $Y\subset X $ be a subset of $X $. An element $x\in Y $ is an interior point of $Y $ if $\exists $ $r>0 $ such that $B(x,r)\subset Y $.

Definition A subset $Y\subset X $ is open if $\text{int} (Y)=Y $.

Theorem Let $(X,d) $ be a metric space. Then $B(x,r) $ for $x\in X $ is open in $X $.

Theorem Let $Y $ be a subset of the metric space $(X,d) $. Then $\text{int} (Y) $ is an open subset of $X $.

Theorem The union of a family of open subsets of $(X,d) $ is an open subset of $X $.

Theorem A subset $U $ of a metric space $(X,d) $ is open iff $U $ is a union of open balls in $X $.

Theorem The intersection of any finite number of open subsets of a metric space is open.

Theorem Let $Y $ be a subspace of a metric space $X $. Then a subset $U $ of $Y $ is open in $Y $ iff $U=V\cap Y $ for some open subset $V $ of $X $.

Remark A subset of $X $ that is open in a subspace of $X $ needs not be open in $X $.

Definition Let $Y\subset (X,d) $ be a subset of the metric space. A point $x \in X $ is an Adherent point of (to) $Y $ if $\forall \;r>0 $, $B(x,r)\cap Y\neq \emptyset $.

Definition Let $Y $ be a subset in a metric space $(X,d) $. The Closure of $Y $, denoted $\overline{Y} $, is the set of all adherent points of $Y $.

Remark $Y\subset \overline{Y} $. Visually, $\overline{Y}=Y+\text{boundary} $.

Definition A subset $Y $ is said to be closed if it is equal to its closure: $Y=\overline{Y} $.

Theorem A subset $Y $ of a metric space $X $ is closed iff the complement of $Y $ is open.

Theorem Let $(X,d) $ be a metric space. Let $Y\subset X $. Then $\overline{\overline{Y}}=\overline{Y} $. That is, $\overline{Y} $ is a closed subset.

Theorem The intersection of any family of closed subsets is closed. The union of any finite number of closed subsets is closed.

Remark The infinite union of closed subsets is not necessarily closed.

Sequences.

Def. A sequence of points $\left\lbrace x_n \right\rbrace _{n=1}^{\infty } $, where $x_n\in X $, is said to converge to $x\in X $ if $\lim_{n\to \infty }d(x_n ,x)=0 $. If so, then we write $x_n \rightarrow x $ as $n\rightarrow \infty $ or simply $\lim_{n\to \infty }x_n =x $.

Lemma The limit of a convergent sequence in a metric space is unique.

Theorem Let $(X,d) $ be a metric space. Let $Y\subseteq X $. Then $x \in X $ is adherent to $Y $ iff there exists a sequence $y_n\in Y $ such that $\lim_{n\to \infty }y_n =x $.

Def. A sequence $\left\lbrace x_n \right\rbrace_{n=1}^{\infty } $ in a metric space $(X,d) $ is a Cauchy sequence if $\lim_{m,n\to \infty }d(x_n,x_m)=0 $. That is, for each $\epsilon >0 $, there exists an $N\in \mathbb{N} $ such that $d(x_n ,x_m)<\epsilon $ for all $n,m\geq N $.

Lemma A convergent sequence is a Cauchy sequence.

Remark Not all Cauchy sequences are convergent sequences.

Lemma If $\left\lbrace x_n \right\rbrace _{n=1}^{\infty } $ is a Cauchy sequence and if there exist a subsequence $\left\lbrace x_{n_k}\right\rbrace_{k=1}^{\infty } $ of $\left\lbrace x_n \right\rbrace_{n=1}^{\infty } $ that converges to $x $, then $\left\lbrace x_n \right\rbrace_{n=1}^{\infty } $ also converges to $x $.

Def. A metric space $(X,d) $ is said to be complete if every Cauchy sequence in $X $ is convergent.

Theorem A closed subspace of a complete metric space is complete.

Theorem A complete subspace $Y $ of a metric space $X$ is closed in $X $.

Def. Let $\left\lbrace f_n \right\rbrace_{n=1}^{\infty } $ be a sequence of functions ($f_n :S\rightarrow X $), where $X $ is a metric space. We say it uniformly converges to $f $, written $f_n \rightarrow f $ uniformly, if for every $\epsilon >0 $, there exists $N\in \mathbb{N} $ such that $d(f_n(s),f(s))<\epsilon $ for all $n\geq N $ and $s \in S $.

Def. Let $\left\lbrace f_n :S\rightarrow X \right\rbrace _{n=1}^{\infty } $ be sequence of functions. We say that it is a Cauchy sequence of functions if for every $\epsilon >0 $, there exists $N \in \mathbb{N} $ such that $d(f_n (s),f_m(s))<\epsilon $ for all $s \in S $ and $n,m\geq N $.

Theorem Let $S $ be a set, and let $X $ be a complete metric space. If $\left\lbrace f_n:S\rightarrow X \right\rbrace_{n=1}^{\infty } $ is a Cauchy sequence of functions, then there exists a function $f:S\rightarrow X $ such that $f_n \rightarrow f $ uniformly.

Remark In the above, we note that we can construct sequences of elements/points by evaluating the functions $f_n $ at a given point. This sequence will also be Cauchy.

Def. Let $X $ be a metric space. A subset $Y\subset X $ is said to be dense in $ X$ if $\overline{Y}=X $.

Theorem (Baire Category theorem) Let $\left\lbrace U_n \right\rbrace_{n=1}^{\infty } $ be a sequence of dense open subsets of a complete metric space $X $. Then $\cap ^{\infty }_{n=1}U_n $ is also dense in $X $.

Remark The Baire Category theorem is NOT true if $X $ is not complete.

Def. Let $X $ be a metric space. A subset $Y\subset X $ is nowhere dense if $\text{int} (\overline{Y})=\emptyset $.

Remark If $Y $ is nowhere dense, then $ (\overline{Y})^{C}$ is open. Claim: $(\overline{Y})^{C} $ is a dense subset.

Corollary Let $\left\lbrace E_n \right\rbrace_{n=1}^{\infty } $ be a sequence of nowhere dense subsets of a complete metric space $ X$. Then $\cup _{n=1}^{\infty }E_n $ has an empty interior.
